## Three interesting side-observations from this run

### 1. C0 reproduced spec 001's λ=0 bitwise-exactly (1.10518)

Same input, same pipeline, same hardware → same 15-decimal bpb. Our sweep infrastructure is deterministic. This is a small thing but worth noting — it means future cross-sweep comparisons (e.g. "did Hessian-SDClip's λ=0 match SWA's C0?") can use bpb as a fingerprint for "same pipeline run."

### 2. Sliding-window eval takes ~12 minutes on 1×H100, not 3

Spec 002 estimated 3 min/config for sliding eval. Reality: **717 seconds on C0 alone.** Execution pivoted mid-run to quant-only for C1-C5 to stay in budget. That was the right call — the kill signal was obvious from quant-only numbers — but the mis-estimate would have cost us $4+ if the configs had shown more ambiguous results.

**Mental model update:** for any future 1×H100 post-training sweep, budget sliding at ~12 min/config. With 6+ configs, skip sliding entirely for the screen and only run it on the winner(s). I had internalized a 2×H100-8×H100 wall-time intuition that doesn't hold on 1×H100.

### 3. Our sweep scripts aren't DDP-aware

Execution tried running `swa_sweep.py` on 8×H100 for an A/B comparison against 1×H100. All 8 ranks raced on GPU 0 (the script hardcodes `device = torch.device("cuda", 0)`), GPU 0 hit 80 GB, the other 7 were idle. Aborted within 4 min, $1.60 wasted.

Not a bug in spec 002's intent — single-GPU is the right call for sweeps — but a limitation we'd run into if we ever wanted multi-GPU screens. ~10 lines of DDP machinery would fix it. Filed for future pattern-level work.

## Why SWA really didn't work (the research-y part)

Three plausible reasons I can't distinguish between:

**A. EMA decay 0.9965 is already the right averaging for this training length.** Over 3849 steps, EMA effectively weights the last ~285 steps at ~63% of total weight. That's a rich, recency-biased continuous average. A 4-point uniform SWA can't out-perform it — you'd need far more snapshots at matched quality to beat EMA.

**B. Our checkpoints span loss-landscape regions that shouldn't be averaged.** Classical SWA assumes snapshots from a flat, low-LR plateau where SGD is already bouncing around a minimum. Our snapshots were at step 1500 (recurrence just activated, high LR, weights still moving fast), 2275 and 3412 (mid-warmdown), and 3849 (near-zero LR). Averaging across those regimes pulls toward a non-minimum. Dropping the 1500 snapshot (C2) did help — +0.018 instead of +0.042 — but not enough.

**C. Quant penalty amplification.** Averaged weights likely have lower magnitude variance than single-checkpoint weights, which interacts with our per-row-std SDClip scale (tighter clip → larger quant error). Didn't directly measure; consistent with the data.

Probably all three are true to some extent. None are actionable without re-architecting spec 000 to emit dense late-warmdown snapshots, which isn't worth doing just to retry SWA.

## Takeaways to bank

1. **Post-training is a dead end on our stack.** Two consecutive kills with clean monotonic-worsening signatures. Stop proposing quant-time modifications unless they're fundamentally different from per-row clip tweaks and weight averaging. Per-group bit allocation is the last possibly-live one but I'm now skeptical.

2. **1×H100 timing intuitions need recalibration.** Sliding eval is 4× longer than I estimated. For multi-config screens, skip sliding and only confirm on winners.

3. **Sweep infrastructure pays off.** The `sweep.py` / `swa_sweep.py` shared pattern is working — cached Hessian + idempotent per-config output + single-GPU execution. Worth adding the ~10 DDP lines if we ever need multi-GPU sweep runs.

4. **Kill fast, shelve cleanly.** Spec 001 + 002 together cost $4.50, took a day, and gave us definitive KILL decisions. That's the right tempo. Don't iterate on dead candidates.
