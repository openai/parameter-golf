what is this challange? OpenAI Model Craft Challenge: Parameter Golf is a challenge to train the best language model that fits in a 16MB artifact and trains in under 10 minutes on 8xH100s, evaluated by compression on the FineWeb validation set (tokenizer-agnostic, bits per byte).
This challenge is heavily inspired by the __NanoGPT Speedrunning__ challenge, where participants compete to train a model that reaches 3.28 FineWeb validation loss as quickly as possible. We're excited to see how optimizing for a parameter-constrained setting pushes people toward unique architectures (test-time compute, aggressive parameter tying, depth recurrence, low-rank training, ...), compression schemes (low precision, QAT, bitnets, novel tokenizers, ...), and other creative submissions (test-time training, long context, megakernels ...).
If you're familiar with __neural scaling laws__, you can consider this challenge a form of L(N) optimization, where the objective is to optimize the lowest loss given a fixed number of parameters (N) unconstrained by data, compute, steps, or architecture. Challenges like the __NanoGPT Speedrun__, which optimizes for a form of L(T) (~lowest time given constrained loss) or the __NanoGPT Slowrun__, which optimizes for L(D) (lowest loss given constrained dataset size), can be thought of as equivalent challenges in this family.
Ideally, we'd allow for submissions to use arbitrary computational resources. But in order to make the challenge not inaccessibly expensive, we're limiting leaderboard submissions to 10 minutes on 8xH100s. However, we'd still love to see submissions that don't meet the compute limitation requirements in our 'Non-record Submissions' section: We're excited to see people push the infinite frontier of parameter limited performance as well.

Great question, and honestly the creativity part is the most interesting angle. Let me build this up from the ground floor.
How a language model works (the 60-second version)
At the most basic level, a language model is a function that takes a sequence of text and predicts what comes next. "The cat sat on the ___" and the model assigns probabilities: "mat" gets a high score, "elephant" gets a low one.
The model is just a big pile of numbers (called parameters or weights). During training, you show it billions of examples of real text, and you adjust those numbers so its predictions get better. The measure of "how wrong are the predictions" is called loss. Lower loss = better model.
Bits per byte is a compression-flavored way to measure that loss. If your model truly "understands" patterns in English, it can predict text efficiently, meaning it compresses well. A perfect predictor would need zero bits. A random guesser would need about 8 bits per byte. Current frontier models land somewhere around 0.7-0.8 bpb.
What the constraints actually mean
The 16MB limit is brutal. For context:
	•	GPT-2 Small (the "tiny" model from 2019) is ~500MB
	•	A single high-res photo on your phone is often bigger than 16MB
	•	You need to fit the model weights AND the code to run it in that budget
So you're trying to cram as much "understanding of English" as possible into roughly the storage of a short video clip.
The 10-minute training window on 8xH100s is generous compute-wise (those are $30k+ GPUs each), but it still means you can't brute-force your way through by training for days.
The key levers participants can pull
Here's where it gets interesting. There are several dimensions to optimize, and they all interact with each other:
Architecture is the shape of the computation. The standard approach (Transformer) is one option, but you could use recurrent networks, state-space models, or hybrids. You can also do tricks like "parameter tying" where you reuse the same weights multiple times (imagine reading a book with the same pair of glasses 12 times, each time noticing different things).
Precision is how many bits you use per parameter. Standard is 32 bits. You can go to 16, 8, 4, even 1 bit (BitNets). Lower precision = more parameters fit in 16MB, but each parameter carries less information. Where's the sweet spot?
Tokenization is how you chop text into pieces before feeding it to the model. "understanding" could be one token, or "under" + "standing", or individual characters. Since the metric is bits per byte (not per token), your tokenizer choice directly affects your score in non-obvious ways.
Training recipe covers learning rate schedules, batch sizes, data ordering, and dozens of other knobs that affect how efficiently those 10 minutes are used.
Test-time compute means doing extra work during evaluation (not training). Like a student who shows their work on an exam, taking more time to reason through each prediction.
Now: why can't AI just solve this?
This is where your instinct is right, and it's a genuinely deep question.
I can explain every technique. I can write the code for any specific architecture. I can even suggest combinations. But here's what I can't do well:
The search space is combinatorially enormous. Imagine you have 50 meaningful design choices, each with 5-10 options. The number of possible configurations is astronomical. No amount of reasoning from first principles tells you whether "4-bit weights + character-level tokenizer + 24 recurrent layers with parameter tying + cosine learning rate schedule" beats "8-bit weights + BPE tokenizer + 12 Transformer layers + warmup-then-linear decay." You have to actually run it and see.
Empirical intuition can't be simulated. The best ML researchers develop a feel for what's going to work, a sense that's built from hundreds of failed experiments. It's like how an experienced cook knows a dish needs acid before tasting it. I can tell you what techniques exist, but I can't tell you which specific combination will be the winning one at this exact constraint point, because nobody has optimized at exactly this constraint before.
Novel combinations are the whole game. The individual techniques (quantization, recurrence, parameter tying) are all known. What's NOT known is the specific cocktail that maximizes performance at exactly 16MB. That's a research contribution. The person who wins will likely combine 3-4 known ideas in a way nobody has tried together, with specific tuning choices informed by dozens of experiments. I can suggest plausible combinations, but I can't run the experiments.
Systems-level interactions are unpredictable. Your tokenizer choice changes how many tokens you process per byte, which changes the optimal model size, which changes how much precision you can afford, which changes training dynamics, which changes optimal learning rate. These cascading interactions make it really hard to reason about from the armchair.
Taste in constraint navigation. This is the subtle one. When you're at a hard constraint boundary, the question becomes "what am I willing to give up and what do I refuse to sacrifice?" That's a judgment call. Some people will bet on more parameters at lower precision. Others will bet on fewer, higher-quality parameters. Others will bet on exotic architectures. The "right" bet depends on intuitions that aren't formalizable.
Think of it like your energy systems background: I can explain thermodynamics and every component of a power plant, but designing a novel plant configuration that maximizes efficiency under a specific set of regulatory and geographic constraints requires an engineer who can iterate, test, and develop intuition about that specific problem. The knowledge is necessary but not sufficient.
The challenge is basically asking: "Given that AI can help with implementation, who has the best taste and intuition for navigating an unexplored design space?" That's a very human skill, and it's exactly what OpenAI is screening for in potential hires.

The scoring in detail

What QWERTY actually encodes
You're right about the history. QWERTY was designed so that commonly co-occurring letter pairs (like "th", "er", "st", "he") are physically separated to prevent jamming. So the keyboard layout is basically an inverted map of English bigram frequencies. Letters far apart on the keyboard tend to appear together often. Letters near each other tend not to.
So your proposal is: we already have this "free" prior about English letter co-occurrence baked into a 150-year-old keyboard layout. Can we inject that as a structural constraint into the model so it doesn't have to waste parameters learning something humans already figured out?
Why the specific QWERTY encoding isn't quite enough
The practical limitation is that bigram frequencies (which two letters tend to follow each other) are the absolute easiest thing for a neural network to learn. A model figures out "t is usually followed by h" within the first few seconds of training. That pattern is so simple it barely costs any parameters.
What eats parameters is the hard stuff: long-range dependencies ("the pronoun 'she' 40 words later refers to 'Maria' at the beginning"), semantic patterns ("after a question, an answer follows"), syntactic structure ("this verb needs to agree with a plural noun three clauses back"). QWERTY tells you nothing about any of that.
Also, the information content of the QWERTY layout is tiny. It's 26 key positions, maybe 100 bytes of data. In a 16MB budget, that's noise.

That's not ridiculous. That's called inductive bias, and it's one of the most important concepts in machine learning. The best architectures are ones that have the right inductive biases baked in. Some real examples of this thinking:
Informed weight initialization. Instead of starting with random weights, you initialize them in a way that already reflects known language statistics. One of the early leaderboard submissions did exactly this: "spectral embed init" was part of a winning submission

, meaning they initialized the embedding matrix using spectral properties of language rather than random values. Your QWERTY idea is the same instinct: don't start from zero, start from what we know.

Fixed attention patterns. In a transformer, every position can attend to every other position. But we know that in English, the most relevant context is usually nearby words, sentence boundaries, and a few long-range references. Sure. Let me build this up step by step with no jargon.
What a tokenizer does
Before the model can process any text, it needs to chop it into pieces. Those pieces are called tokens. The tokenizer is the knife.
Think of it like this. You have the sentence: "The cat sat on the mat."
You could chop it into individual letters: T, h, e, space, c, a, t... That gives you ~24 pieces. Each piece carries very little meaning on its own.
You could chop it into words: "The", "cat", "sat", "on", "the", "mat". That's 6 pieces. Each piece carries a lot of meaning.
You could chop it into something in between: "Th", "e", "cat", "sat", "on", "the", "mat". This is roughly what BPE tokenizers do. They find the most common letter combinations and merge them into single tokens.
The baseline model uses a vocabulary of 1,024 tokens. That's a dictionary of 1,024 "chunks" that the model knows. Every piece of text has to be spelled using only those 1,024 chunks. Common words like "the" are probably a single chunk. Rare words get broken into multiple chunks.
Designing a custom tokenizer means choosing which 1,024 chunks to put in your dictionary. The standard approach is purely statistical: scan the training data, find the most common pairs of characters, merge them, repeat. But you could be smarter about it.
What embedding initialization does
Once you've chopped text into tokens, the model needs to convert each token into a list of numbers. That's the embedding. Token #47 might become [0.23, -0.81, 0.44, ... 512 numbers total]. At the start of training, these numbers are random.
The model then learns to adjust these numbers so that tokens with similar meanings end up with similar number-lists. After training, "cat" and "dog" would have similar embeddings, while "cat" and "democracy" would have very different ones.
Embedding initialization is the starting point for those numbers. Normally it's random noise. But what if you gave the model a head start?
The "front-loading" idea explained
Imagine you're hiring someone to organize a massive library. They have 10 minutes and limited shelf space (that's the model's training time and parameter budget).
The default approach: You drop them into a completely unmarked library. No labels, no categories, no signs. They have to figure out everything from scratch. "OK, these seem to be about cooking... these seem to be fiction... wait, this one is also fiction but it was in the wrong pile..." They'll get some organization done in 10 minutes, but they're spending a lot of their time on basics.
The front-loaded approach: Before they start, you put up signs. "Fiction is on the left wall. Science on the right. Alphabetical within each section. Here's a list of the 100 most checked-out books, give them the most accessible spots." Now when they start their 10 minutes, they skip all the basic organizational work and go straight to the nuanced stuff: "Should I sub-categorize science fiction by era or by author? Where does a cooking memoir go, cooking or memoir?"
That's what front-loading means. You give the model a head start so it doesn't waste its limited time and space on things you already know.
Concrete examples of what this looks like
Tokenizer front-loading: The default tokenizer picks its 1,024 chunks purely by frequency in the data. But you know things about English that frequency alone doesn't capture:
	•	You know "un-" is a prefix that reverses meaning. If "happy" is a token and "unhappy" is a token, the model has to independently learn what both mean. But if you designed the tokenizer so "un" and "happy" are separate tokens, the model can learn the "un = reverse" rule once and apply it everywhere. That's more efficient use of your 1,024 slots.
	•	You know that "ing", "ed", "tion", "ness" are suffixes that transform words in predictable ways. A tokenizer designed around morphological structure (roots + affixes) might let the model learn English more efficiently than one designed purely around character frequency.
	•	You know that punctuation marks function very differently from letters. A period means "sentence boundary." A comma means "pause/list." Giving these their own dedicated tokens (rather than merging them with adjacent letters) encodes structural information for free.
Embedding front-loading: Instead of random starting numbers, you set them up so the model already "knows" some things on step zero:
	•	You could make "a" and "A" start with nearly identical embeddings, so the model doesn't have to spend training time discovering that uppercase and lowercase versions of the same letter are related.
	•	You could make vowels start with embeddings that are similar to each other (and different from consonants), encoding a basic phonetic distinction before training begins.
	•	You could make digits (0-9) start clustered together, separate from letters, so the model already "knows" that numbers are a different category.
	•	You could use known word frequency data to set up the initial embedding space so that common tokens get more "room" in the number-space than rare tokens.
None of these things individually is a big deal. The model WOULD learn all of this from data eventually. But "eventually" might take 3 of your 10 minutes. If you front-load it, the model gets those 3 minutes back to learn harder things like grammar, meaning, and long-range context.
The budget analogy
Think of 16MB as a financial budget.
The embedding table is like rent. It's a fixed cost that scales with your vocabulary size. A 1,024-token vocabulary at 512 dimensions in 16-bit precision costs about 1MB just for embeddings. That's 6% of your budget gone before the model does anything intelligent.
A poorly designed tokenizer is like overpaying for rent. You have 1,024 slots, and if some of them are wasted on redundant or unhelpful chunks, you're paying for shelf space you don't need.
Front-loading the embeddings is like furnishing your apartment with stuff from your parents' house instead of buying everything new. You didn't pay for the knowledge, but you benefit from it.
The "hard stuff" the model needs to spend its remaining budget on is things like: understanding that "bank" means something different in "river bank" versus "bank account," knowing that a paragraph about cooking is probably followed by ingredient amounts, predicting that a legal document uses formal structure. The theory is that your brain doesn't actually read every letter. It sees the bold prefix, recognizes the word, and skips the rest. Your brain is "autocompleting" each word from a partial signal. The endings are redundant because once you see "br" followed by a tall letter in the context of a sentence about an animal, your brain already knows it's "brown."
Why this maps onto the challenge beautifully
You're identifying something that information theory already confirms: not all parts of a word carry equal information.
The first letter of a word is the hardest to predict. It could be anything. But once you see "eleph," the model (and your brain) knows with near-certainty that "ant" follows. The beginning of a word carries high information (high entropy, hard to guess). The ending carries low information (low entropy, easy to guess).
This is true at every level:
	•	First letter of a word: hardest to predict
	•	Middle letters: easier
	•	Last letters: often almost free ("tion", "ing", "ment" are nearly automatic)
	•	Similarly, the first word of a sentence is harder than the fifth word
	•	The first sentence of a paragraph is harder than the third
How you could actually apply this

Unequal attention allocation. Right now, the transformer treats every token position equally. Every token gets the same amount of computation. But if the beginnings of words are where all the hard prediction work happens, why not allocate MORE model capacity to those positions and LESS to the predictable suffixes?
Imagine a model that runs 12 layers of processing on the first token of each word but only 4 layers on the last token. You're spending computation (which is abundant) instead of parameters (which are scarce). The model is essentially doing Bionic Reading in reverse: working hard on the prefix, coasting on the suffix.
Morphological tokenizer design. This connects back to our previous conversation. If you designed your tokenizer to separate word stems from endings:
Instead of: ["the", "elephant", "walked", "slowly"]
You tokenize as: ["the", "eleph", "-ant", "walk", "-ed", "slow", "-ly"]
Now the model has a small set of suffix tokens ("-ed", "-ing", "-tion", "-ly", "-ment", "-ness", etc.) that it can learn to predict very cheaply because they follow predictable rules. The "hard" prediction budget goes toward stems and word-initial tokens, where the real information lives. You've essentially built Bionic Reading's insight into the tokenizer itself.

Glad you're getting into it. Let me walk through the main categories of techniques people use, with concrete examples for each. I'll keep it in plain English as much as possible.
1. Making each parameter cheaper (Quantization)
This is the most straightforward lever. Every number in your model takes up space. How much space depends on the precision you store it at.
Full precision (32-bit): Each parameter is stored with extreme accuracy. Like measuring your height as 5 feet, 10 inches, 3/16ths. You have 16MB / 4 bytes = 4 million parameters max.
Half precision (16-bit): Good enough for most purposes. 5 feet, 10 inches. You get 8 million parameters.
INT8 (8-bit): Slight quality loss. 5'10". You get 16 million parameters. This is what the baseline uses.
INT4 (4-bit): Noticeable quality loss per parameter, but you get 32 million parameters. If those extra parameters learn enough to compensate for the precision loss, you win.
INT2 or binary (1-2 bit): Each parameter is basically "positive, negative, or zero." Extreme compression. A model called BitNet showed this can work at scale. You'd get 64-128 million parameters in 16MB, but each one is barely a nudge rather than a precise value. The question is whether sheer quantity beats quality.
Think of it like painting. Full precision is oil paint with perfect color mixing. INT4 is a box of 16 crayons. Binary is just black and white. But if you can use 32x more black-and-white strokes than oil brushstrokes, maybe the black-and-white painting is actually more detailed overall.
Quantization-aware training (QAT) is training the model while it knows it will be compressed. Instead of training at full precision and then crushing it down (which loses quality), the model trains with the constraint from the start and learns to work within it. Like learning to paint with crayons from day one rather than learning with oil paints and then being handed crayons for the exam.
2. Sharing parameters (doing more with less)
Instead of having unique weights everywhere, you reuse the same weights for multiple purposes.
Tied embeddings (already in baseline). The model has two big tables: one that converts words into numbers at the input, and one that converts numbers back into word predictions at the output. Tying them means using ONE table for both jobs. That instantly saves ~1MB in the baseline. It works because the skills are related: knowing what a word "means" (input embedding) is closely connected to knowing when to predict that word (output embedding).
Layer tying / depth recurrence. A normal 12-layer transformer has 12 unique sets of weights. But what if layers 1-4, 5-8, and 9-12 all shared the same weights? You'd have 4 unique layers that get applied 3 times each. Your model acts like a 12-layer model (deep, powerful) but only stores 4 layers (compact). Think of it like reading the same book three times. Each time you read it, you notice different things even though the book is the same. The "recurrence" means the model passes through the same weights multiple times, extracting different information each pass because the input to each pass is different.
Cross-layer parameter sharing. You could share just certain components across layers. Maybe all 12 layers share the same "key" and "value" projections (the parts that decide what information to look for) but have unique "query" projections (the parts that decide what to ask). This is a middle ground between full sharing and no sharing.
Grouped Query Attention (GQA, already in baseline). Standard attention has, say, 8 "attention heads." Each head has its own query, key, and value matrices. GQA says: let 8 query heads share only 4 key/value heads. Two queries share each key/value pair. You save parameters on the key/value side without losing much on the query side. The baseline uses this: 8 attention heads but only 4 KV heads.
3. Making the architecture itself more efficient
These are structural changes to how the model is wired together.
Low-rank factorization. Imagine you have a weight matrix that's 512 x 512. That's 262,144 parameters. But what if the "real information" in that matrix could be captured by two smaller matrices: 512 x 32 and 32 x 512? That's only 32,768 parameters, an 8x savings. This works when the matrix is "low rank," meaning most of its information lies along a few key dimensions. LoRA (used in fine-tuning) is based on this principle. For this challenge, you could train with low-rank constraints from the start.
Think of it like this. A 512x512 matrix can represent any transformation from 512 inputs to 512 outputs. But most useful transformations aren't that complex. A sentence's meaning can usually be captured in far fewer dimensions than the raw representation suggests. Low-rank factorization exploits that by saying "I know this matrix is simpler than it looks, so I'll store it as a simpler object."
Mixture of Experts (MoE). Instead of one big feedforward layer that processes every token, you have, say, 4 smaller "expert" layers and a tiny "router" that decides which expert handles each token. Token about math? Expert 2. Token about cooking? Expert 4. Each token only activates 1 expert, so you use 1/4 the compute per token, but you store 4 experts. The trick for this challenge is that you're constrained on storage, not compute. So MoE might not help directly unless combined with other tricks. But if the router is smart enough, each expert can specialize and be simpler (lower rank) than a single generalist layer.
State Space Models (like Mamba). Transformers have a quadratic problem: attention compares every token to every other token. A 1,000-token sequence means 1,000,000 comparisons. SSMs instead process tokens sequentially, maintaining a compressed "state" that summarizes everything seen so far. This is much faster for long sequences and can be more parameter-efficient. The tradeoff is that they sometimes miss the long-range connections that attention handles well. Hybrid architectures (some attention layers + some SSM layers) try to get both benefits.
Sparse attention. Instead of every token attending to every other token (full attention), you define patterns. Maybe each token only attends to its 128 nearest neighbors plus every 64th token in the past. This reduces computation without proportionally reducing quality, because most useful context is nearby anyway. It's like how you read a book: you mostly pay attention to the current paragraph, occasionally flip back to check something from a few pages ago, but you rarely re-read page 3 while you're on page 200.
4. Training faster (getting more learning per minute)
With only 10 minutes on 8 GPUs, every second counts.
Better optimizers. The standard optimizer (Adam) keeps track of two "momentum" values for every parameter. The Muon optimizer (used in the baseline) uses a different strategy that can converge faster for certain architectures. Others like Lion, Sophia, or SOAP each have different tradeoffs between per-step cost and convergence speed. The challenge is finding which optimizer squeezes the most learning out of 10 minutes.
Think of optimizers like hiking strategies for finding the lowest valley in a mountain range. Adam takes careful, measured steps and remembers which direction has been working. SGD just walks downhill. Muon uses the geometry of the landscape to take smarter steps. With only 10 minutes of hiking, the strategy matters a lot.
Learning rate scheduling. How aggressively should the model learn? Start fast and slow down (cosine decay)? Warm up slowly then go fast then slow down (warmup + cosine)? The right schedule can make a huge difference in 10 minutes. Going too fast causes the model to overshoot. Going too slow wastes precious training time on tiny updates.
Batch size optimization. A bigger batch means each training step sees more examples, so the gradient (the "which way is downhill" signal) is more reliable. But it also means fewer total steps in 10 minutes. Smaller batches give more steps but noisier signals. There's an optimal point that depends on the model and hardware.
FlashAttention. This isn't an architectural change but a systems optimization. Standard attention does a lot of unnecessary memory reads/writes. FlashAttention rearranges the same math to be much more GPU-friendly. Same result, 2-3x faster. It's like reorganizing a factory floor so workers don't walk as far between stations. Same product, faster throughput.
Fused kernels / megakernels. GPUs are fastest when they do one big operation rather than many small ones. Normally, each layer does: attention, then normalize, then feedforward, then normalize, each as a separate GPU operation with overhead between them. A fused kernel combines several of these into a single GPU operation, eliminating the overhead. This is pure engineering optimization and it's grueling work, but it can buy you significantly more training steps in 10 minutes.
5. Compression after training
Once the model is trained, you need to squeeze it into 16MB.
Weight pruning. Many parameters end up near zero after training. If a weight is 0.0001, it's barely doing anything. Set it to exactly 0 and store only the non-zero weights. A model with 50% sparsity (half the weights pruned) stores roughly half the data. Structured pruning (removing entire rows/columns) is even more efficient to store and run.
Weight clustering. Instead of storing the exact value of every weight, you find, say, 256 representative values (centroids) and map each weight to its nearest centroid. Now each weight is an 8-bit index (0-255) rather than a 32-bit float. This is basically k-means clustering applied to weight values.
Entropy coding (zlib, etc.). After quantization, the weight values aren't uniformly distributed. Some values appear much more often than others. Entropy coding (like zip compression) exploits this to squeeze out a few more percent. The baseline already uses zlib on top of INT8 quantization.
Knowledge distillation. Train a big, expensive "teacher" model first (maybe using all 10 minutes). Then use the teacher's predictions to train a much smaller "student" model. The student learns from the teacher's soft probabilities (which contain more information than the raw data) and can sometimes match the teacher's performance in a fraction of the size. For this challenge, you could spend 7 minutes training a large model and 3 minutes distilling it into a 16MB student. Whether this beats spending all 10 minutes on the small model directly is an empirical question.
6. Getting more out of evaluation time
Remember, evaluation has a separate 10-minute budget and methods are unrestricted

.
Test-time training (TTT). As the model reads the evaluation text, it updates its own weights on the tokens it has already seen. It's like a student who studies DURING the exam based on the questions they've already answered. Only backward-looking TTT is allowed, meaning the model can adapt on tokens already graded but not look at future tokens. 
GitHub

 This is currently one of the most powerful techniques, with TTT-based submissions competitive with or beating non-TTT approaches.
Sliding window evaluation. Instead of processing the eval text in one shot, you use overlapping windows. Each token gets predicted multiple times from different contexts, and you average the predictions. More computation, better estimates, same model.
Ensemble at inference. If your 16MB can fit two different 8MB models instead of one 16MB model, you could average their predictions at eval time. Two diverse models often make complementary errors, so the average is better than either alone.
How these all interact
The beautiful (and maddening) thing about this challenge is that all these techniques interact with each other in complex ways. Going to 4-bit precision lets you fit more layers, but more layers take longer to train, which might not fit in 10 minutes, unless you use FlashAttention and fused kernels to speed things up, but those require engineering effort that could have been spent on a better tokenizer, which might have given you more improvement anyway.
That's the game. And it's why the challenge rewards creativity and taste alongside technical skill.

The most common letters in English by frequency:

e  — 12.7%    → give it key 3 (one token)

The remaining 18 letters (r, d, l, c, u, m, w, f, g, y, p, b, v, k, j, x, q, z) each cost 2 tokens. But many of these are rare. Letters like x, q, z barely appear.
You just reinvented Huffman coding
In 1952, David Huffman solved exactly this problem: given symbols with different frequencies, what's the optimal way to assign variable-length codes so the average code length is minimized? His answer: give short codes to frequent symbols and long codes to rare symbols.
Your instinct, "common letter 'a' should be cheaper than rare letter 'x'", is literally Huffman's insight. You got there from phone keyboards instead of information theory, but the destination is the same.
Taking it even further
Once you accept variable-length encoding, you don't have to stop at letters. You could give single-token codes to the most common PAIRS or even whole words:

" the " = single token T    (one token for the most common word)
" and " = single token A    (one token)
"e"     = single token 3    (one token for most common letter)
"t"     = single token 8    (one token)
...
"q"     = two tokens (7, 4) (rare letter, longer code is fine)
"z"     = two tokens (9, 4) (almost never appears)
And now you've reinvented... BPE tokenization. Which is what the baseline already uses. BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) does exactly this: it finds the most common pairs and merges them into single tokens, iteratively, until it reaches the desired vocabulary size. The most common chunks get short representations, rare ones stay long.
So is your idea pointless?
No. Here's why. BPE optimizes for the average case across all text. Your idea has a different angle: you're thinking about the structure of the encoding itself. BPE treats the vocabulary as a flat list of 1,024 arbitrary chunks. Your encoding has structure:
	•	There's a hierarchy: key group first, then position
	•	The model can learn rules like "after any key-digit, the position digit is always 1-4"
	•	The model can learn that position tokens are MUCH more predictable than key tokens
This structure makes individual predictions easier, even though there are more of them. BPE gives you one hard prediction per token. Your scheme gives you alternating easy and hard predictions: the key is hard (what letter comes next?), but the position is easy (given the key, which letter on that key?).
That alternating easy/hard pattern could actually help the model learn faster, because the easy predictions provide "free" gradient signal that stabilizes training. It's like giving a student a test where every other question is a freebie. Their confidence builds, they learn the hard questions better.
The practical experiment
Here's what I'd actually test. Three configurations:
Config A (baseline): Standard 1,024-token BPE. This is what everyone uses. ~1.12-1.14 BPB currently.
Config B (your encoding, unoptimized): Frequency-optimized variable-length letter encoding. ~15 symbols. Tiny embedding table. Use the freed-up bytes for an extra transformer layer.
Config C (hybrid): Use your encoding for rare words that BPE can't handle efficiently, but keep BPE's single tokens for the most common words ("the", "and", "is", "of"). Best of both worlds: common stuff is fast, rare stuff uses your structured encoding.
Config C is probably the most promising because it's additive. You're not throwing away what BPE does well, you're supplementing it with a smarter fallback for the tokens BPE handles poorly.
