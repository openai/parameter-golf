A reframe that came up while not at the desk:

**The side memory is a training-shortcut prior, not a recall mechanism.** At 200 steps the model under-trains. Static N-gram embeds dataset statistics directly in the artifact, freeing the model to focus its limited training time on the residual. At H100 20k-step the model trains enough that the prior contributes little — that's why the H100 transfer estimate is small (-0.01 to -0.02).

If that's the right frame, the natural extensions are not "more N-gram variants" but **other training-shortcut priors** that the model can lean on. Categories:
- Token frequency priors (unigram, already inside the N-gram fallback)
- Sub-token co-occurrence (substring N-grams, not full token N-grams)
- POS-tag priors (would need a tagger; not here)
- Document-type embeddings (e.g. "is this code, prose, or math?" cluster IDs)
- Distilled-sub-vocab priors from a stronger teacher

The brief's (d) candidate (dendritic codebook) IS one such prior — but the rank=5 finding from UU#4 suggests an even simpler form would work: **16 keys × full d_model attention readout**, with keys learned from training (or initialized from k-means on hidden states). The 32-bucket sign-hash HSM had gradient sparsity because each bucket's value was rarely updated. With 16 dense keys + softmax attention, every key gets gradient on every token (via softmax mixing). That's the missing piece.
